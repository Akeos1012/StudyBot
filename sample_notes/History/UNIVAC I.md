### First Commercially Available Computer

**Univac I** - Universal Automatic Computer it was the first commercially produced electronic digital computer in the United States and one of the earliest computers designed for business and data processing.

It was developed by **J. Presper Eckert and John Mauchly**, the same engineers behind ENIAC, and was completed in the early 1950s after their company was acquired by Remington Rand. The first UNIVAC I system was delivered to the **U.S. Census Bureau in 1951**.

Unlike earlier experimental machines, UNIVAC I was designed for real-world applications such as government and business data processing. It gained public attention in 1952 when it successfully predicted the result of the U.S. presidential election on live television.

- First commercially produced electronic computer in the U.S.
- Built for business and government data processing, not just experiments
- Used magnetic tape for storing and processing large amounts of data
- Contained thousands of vacuum tubes for computation
- Famous for predicting the 1952 U.S. presidential election on TV
- Helped launch the era of commercial computing systems

Although **UNIVAC I** marked the beginning of modern commercial computing in the 1950s, the idea of using machines to automate calculation is much older.

Long before electronic computers or even mechanical systems like the Analytical Engine, ancient engineers built analog devices to model mathematical and astronomical problems.

One of the earliest known examples is the **[[Antikythera Mechanism]]**, an ancient Greek device often described as the world’s first known analog computer. It was used to predict astronomical positions and eclipses using complex gear systems, showing that the idea of “computing machines” existed over 2,000 years before modern computers.
