The idea of coding has been around since the 1800s. It all began with **Punch Cards** used in the textile industry. 

### 1801 - Punch Cards and the First Programmable Machines

One of the earliest foundations of programming appeared in the textile industry.

French inventor **Joseph Marie Jacquard** created the **Jacquard Loom**, a weaving machine controlled using **Punch Cards**. Each card contained holes that represented instructions controlling how patterns were woven into fabric.

By changing the sequence of cards, workers could produce different patterns without rebuilding the machine.

This introduced a powerful idea:
<u>Machines can change behavior based on instructions.</u>

### 1822 - Charles Babbage and Mechanical Computation

Inspired by programmable systems and mathematical automation, **Charles Babbage** designed the **Difference Engine**, a mechanical machine intended to calculate mathematical tables automatically.

Later, in **1833**, Babbage proposed a more advanced machine called the **[[Analytical Engine]]**.

---

### 1842 - Ada Lovelace and the First Computer Algorithm

In 1842, Italian engineer **Luigi Menabrea** published a paper describing Babbage’s Analytical Engine.

**Ada Lovelace** translated the paper into English and added extensive notes that became more influential than the original article.

Lovelace realized the machine could do more than calculations.

She proposed that if numbers could represent symbols, a machine could potentially process music, text, and other forms of information.

Her additions included:

- Notes A–G explaining the Analytical Engine
- **Note G**, a step-by-step method for calculating **[[Bernoulli numbers]]**
- One of the earliest published computer [[Algorithms]] intended for a machine

For this work, Ada Lovelace is widely recognized as the **world’s first computer programmer**, although historians note that Babbage also created earlier unpublished programs for the engine.

## From Punch Cards to [[Binary Code]]

The Analytical Engine never became a real machine in its time, but its ideas survived.

Over the next century, computers evolved from mechanical systems and punch cards into electronic machines that stored and processed instructions using **Binary Code**.

Instead of holes in cards, modern computers represent information using:

- **0 = Off**
- **1 = On**

Programs, text, images, and applications are ultimately executed through binary instructions.

This is why coding today can be traced back to a simple historical idea:

**A machine follows instructions.**
